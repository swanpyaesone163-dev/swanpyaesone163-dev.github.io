Presentation Preparation

Anticipated Q&A — Full Question Bank

Every question below has been rated for how well the current proposal can answer it. Green means you have a strong answer ready. Amber means you have relevant information but need to frame it carefully. Red means you will need to be honest about the gap — a confident acknowledgement is better than a weak deflection.

💡

Presentation principle: For amber and red questions, the strongest response is always: acknowledge the gap honestly, explain why it exists at this stage of development, and describe the concrete next step you would take to resolve it. Evaluators at this level reward intellectual honesty and structured thinking far more than defensive overreach.

1
Financial Viability
Watch Out
Where does the startup capital come from, and how much do you actually need?
You can name the sources — IREDA loans (up to 70% of CBG plant costs), GOBARdhan grants, and CSR partnerships — and explain that exact capital requirements depend on the scale of the Phase I digester, which is determined by the target community size. However, you do not currently have a rupee figure. Be upfront: the next step is a feasibility study with an environmental engineering consultant to estimate digester capacity and cost per tonne of waste.
Even though you have the funding sources identified, not having an actual figure will be noticed. Prepare a rough ballpark — e.g. community biogas digesters in India range from ₹5–50 lakhs depending on capacity — so you're not completely without a number.
What is the break-even point — how long until Phase I is financially self-sustaining?
You can point to the Indore GOBARdhan and Pune SWaCH models as comparable benchmarks, and note that the multiple revenue streams (compost, biogas, recyclables, tipping fees, user fees) reduce the time to break-even compared to single-stream models. However, a specific month or year estimate for your model does not yet exist in the proposal. Again — the honest answer is that this requires a community-specific feasibility study.
This is one of the most predictable questions in any NGO pitch. Even a conservative estimate framed as "approximately 18–36 months based on comparable models" is far better than "we haven't calculated it yet."
Why do you have so many revenue streams? Isn't that overly complex to manage?
The diversification is intentional and each stream activates at a different phase — compost, biogas, and recyclables from Phase I; tipping fees via a municipal MoU once the system is proven; EPR registration once recycling volumes are documented; carbon credits in the medium term requiring auditor verification. No single stream is expected to function from day one. The complexity is phased, not simultaneous.
What happens to the whole model if Phase I doesn't generate enough revenue?
The model is explicitly designed so that Phase II does not begin until Phase I has stabilised — this is what the KPI framework is for. If Phase I underperforms, the tipping fee arrangement with the municipal body provides a floor revenue that doesn't depend on market prices for compost or recyclables. The model does not proceed to Phase II on a fixed timeline; it proceeds on a performance threshold.
2
Scale, Target Community & Pilot Location
Watch Out
What is your target community size, and do you have a specific site in mind?
You have five shortlisted cities with strong justifications and Pune as the top recommendation — this demonstrates research rigour. However, no specific ward, neighbourhood, or community-level partner within a city has been identified. The honest answer: the next implementation step is community-level scoping within the pilot city, which requires local NGO partnerships or municipal introductions.
People will push past "Pune" and ask "which part of Pune?" Prepare one hypothetical answer — e.g. "a ward with a resident population of 5,000–10,000 and existing SWaCH operations, such as those in Kothrud or Hadapsar" — so the answer doesn't stall at the city level.
Why Pune specifically over other cities?
Pune hosts SWaCH, India's first and longest-running waste picker cooperative (3,500+ workers, 150,000 households daily, operational since 2007). It has an established network of decentralised biodigesters, a proven culture of citizen participation in waste management, proximity to IIT Pune and multiple management institutions for M&E partnerships, and a dense CSR-active corporate base. No other Indian city combines all five factors at this level of maturity.
How many tonnes of waste does a biogas digester need to be viable?
Small community-scale digesters typically require a minimum of 0.5–1 tonne of organic waste per day to produce meaningful biogas output. A community of 1,000–2,000 households generating the Indian urban average of 300–400g of organic waste per person per day would produce well above this threshold. The proposal's community-level targeting is therefore technically appropriate even for relatively small pilot areas.
3
Waste Picker Integration
Well Covered
Won't this NGO just displace the informal waste pickers who already operate in the area?
This is the most important ethical question in the proposal, and the model is specifically designed to prevent displacement. Every informal waste picker in the catchment is registered, given a formal ID, and onboarded as a paid collection coordinator — using their existing route knowledge and sorting skills as professional assets, not discarding them. The Pune SWaCH model did exactly this for 3,500 workers over 20 years and is the direct precedent for this design.
Why cash payments rather than a product credit system — isn't a credit system more innovative?
A product credit system is conceptually interesting but creates a practical barrier: waste pickers need cash for daily household expenses, and converting compost or biogas credits into usable value adds friction and opacity. Cash via Jan Dhan bank transfer is transparent, immediately usable, and auditable. The cooperative savings structure on top — modelled on KKPKP in Pune — provides the financial depth and innovation that a credit system aims for, but without the daily liquidity problem.
How do you stop a trained and now-formal worker from leaving for a better-paying job once they're skilled up?
The model addresses this through cooperative membership benefits (credit access, savings returns) and a structured career ladder — sorter to coordinator to supervisor to plant operator — that creates genuine upward mobility within the system. However, there is no guarantee, and at this stage no specific wage benchmarking against competing employers has been done.
This is a genuinely hard question. Acknowledge that attrition is a real risk in any skills-development model, and that the cooperative structure is designed to make the NGO's offer competitive not just on wage but on financial security, status, and career growth. Don't oversell the certainty.
4
Community Participation & Behaviour Change
Well Covered
How do you actually get households to segregate their waste? Awareness campaigns usually don't work.
Agreed — and the model is designed around this exact insight. Awareness is necessary but not sufficient. The three-channel approach (social media, door-to-door, events) is paired with physical infrastructure: colour-coded bin sets are distributed to every household during the first door-to-door visit, so participation has no additional cost or friction. The Ambikapur SHG model and the Haridwar UNICEF pilot both demonstrated that sustained household visits over multiple weeks — not one-time campaigns — are what produce lasting segregation behaviour.
Where do the student volunteers come from, and is that supply reliable?
NSS (National Service Scheme) units are embedded in colleges and universities across India, with over 40 lakh enrolled students who are required to complete community service hours each year. This is an institutionally mandated, annually renewed volunteer supply — not dependent on individual motivation or ad-hoc recruitment. The NGO partners with the NSS unit at the nearest college to the pilot community, which is a standard, well-established collaboration.
What if a specific community is resistant — especially if there are political or caste dynamics at play?
The model recognises that community-level resistance is a real and context-specific risk. The field evidence shows that deploying local community champions — people from within the community who are trained as advocates — is significantly more effective than outside-in campaigns. The proposal includes this as a design element. However, a community entry strategy for navigating specific political or caste dynamics at the pilot site has not yet been designed.
This question may come from someone with local knowledge of your pilot city. Don't pretend the challenge doesn't exist. A strong answer acknowledges it and names a community-entry process: initial meetings with local elected representatives (Councillor/Panchayat), self-help group leaders, and women's groups before any outreach begins.
5
M&E Framework & Academic Partnerships
Well Covered
How will you actually measure whether this is working?
Each phase has specific, measurable KPIs: Phase I tracks waste diverted (tonnes/month), biogas produced (m³), and revenue vs. cost ratio. Phase II tracks fuel cost reduction and student engagement. Phase III tracks welfare scheme enrollment rates and before/after awareness scores administered by academic partners. Phase IV tracks employment placement, income at 6 and 12 months, and retention rates. No phase proceeds until its predecessor meets its defined KPI thresholds.
Have J-PAL or any of the academic partners actually agreed to work with you?
At this stage, the academic partners named represent the most appropriate and realistic institutional options given their documented India-based development evaluation mandates — not confirmed partnerships. The next step in implementation would be formal outreach and MoU discussions. J-PAL South Asia, 3ie, and IIM Kozhikode all have explicit frameworks for NGO collaboration and regularly partner with emerging organisations on evaluation design.
Be transparent that these are proposed partners, not confirmed ones. Framing it as "these are the institutions we will approach first, because they have existing mandates and India-based operations, and this is the type of evaluation design we would propose to them" is accurate and credible.
How do you know when a phase has "stabilised" before starting the next?
Phase I is considered stable when it achieves a revenue-to-operational-cost ratio of 1.0 or above (i.e., break-even or better) for a sustained period — a threshold that would be set in the feasibility study. This means Phase II does not begin on a calendar schedule; it begins when the economic evidence supports it. The KPI framework exists precisely to prevent the model from overextending before its foundation is secure.
6
Hard Questions — Gaps That Remain
Prepare Carefully
What happens if the biogas digester breaks down? Is there a contingency plan?
This is a genuine gap in the current proposal. A technical failure at the Phase I digester would disrupt biogas supply to Phase II schools and reduce compost and energy revenue. An honest answer: at this stage, the model does not include a redundancy or maintenance protocol. The immediate next design step would be to include a service agreement with a biogas equipment supplier, a maintenance reserve fund within the operational budget, and a contingency arrangement (e.g. temporary LPG supply to schools) during downtime.
Do not try to wing a technical answer if you don't have one. Acknowledge the gap, frame it as a design element for the implementation phase, and note the category of solutions (maintenance contracts, reserve funds, backup supply). This is credible. Guessing at technical specifications is not.
What is your plan for gender inclusion? Who specifically is this helping among women?
Currently the model does not have an explicit gender strategy. The waste picker sector in India is overwhelmingly female — most rag pickers are women — so integration inherently benefits women, and Phase III's adult education programme is designed to reach anyone who cannot access welfare benefits. However, there is no explicit women-led participation design, no mention of self-help group integration as an entry point, and no gender disaggregation in any of the KPIs.
This is one of the most likely questions from a socially aware evaluator. A strong honest answer: "We recognise this is a gap. In the refined implementation design, we would integrate with existing women's SHG networks as the primary entry point for Phase III, use gender-disaggregated KPIs in our M&E framework, and ensure that waste picker onboarding specifically targets and supports women workers who are currently unrecognised in the informal sector."
What legal or organisational structure will this NGO take? Who is behind it?
The proposal does not yet name a founding team or legal structure. For a presentation, this is one of the most immediately deflating gaps — evaluators want to know who is accountable for delivering what is being described. At minimum, you should be ready to state: who the core team is, what their relevant background is, and what registration pathway is planned (Section 8 company, trust, or society under Indian law).
This cannot be answered by research — only you can fill this in. If this is a student project, frame it as: "This is a concept developed as part of [course/programme], and in an actual implementation context, [describe the governance structure you'd set up]." Do not leave this question unanswered at a live presentation.
What's your plan if the municipal corporation moves into your catchment area and takes over waste collection?
This is a real operational threat with no current mitigation strategy in the proposal. The partial answer: if the MoU-based tipping fee arrangement is secured from the beginning, the NGO becomes a service provider to the municipal body rather than a competitor — making displacement far less likely. But this depends on the MoU being signed before the system is operational, which requires political groundwork.
Frame this as: "This is why formalising the relationship with the municipality through a tipping fee MoU is a priority in Phase I — it converts a competitive threat into a contractual partnership." It's not a complete answer, but it's a directionally correct one.
Before You Present

Presentation Notes

Beyond knowing the content, how you handle the Q&A matters as much as the proposal itself. These notes address the most common presentation mistakes for this type of NGO concept pitch.

🎯
Lead with the loop, not the phases

The circular self-reinforcing system is your most distinctive idea. Open with it — the diagram, the logic, the why — before walking through the phases. Evaluators who understand the loop will follow the phases with context; those who see the phases first may never grasp the larger design.

💰
Name a rough number for startup cost

Even a range — "community-scale biodigesters in India typically cost ₹5–50 lakhs depending on capacity" — is far better than no number. Saying "we haven't calculated it" in response to a financial question signals insufficient preparation, even if the research is otherwise strong.

🤝
Own the gaps, don't defend them

For the three "Still Open" weaknesses — no cost projections, no gender strategy, no legal structure — the strongest possible response is: acknowledge it, name the reason it doesn't exist yet (concept stage vs. implementation stage), and state the concrete next step. This is more credible than a defensive answer.

🏭
SWaCH is your anchor example — use it confidently

Pune's SWaCH cooperative is a 20-year, 3,500-worker, 150,000-household success story that mirrors your model's core design. When challenged on feasibility, worker integration, or financial sustainability, return to SWaCH. It is your strongest piece of real-world evidence.

📋
Distinguish "proposed" from "confirmed"

Academic partners (J-PAL, 3ie), pilot cities (Pune), and government scheme linkages are proposed, not confirmed. Be clear about this distinction — evaluators will check, and overclaiming damages credibility on everything else. "We have identified and would approach..." is always the safer and more honest framing.

👩
Prepare a gender response before the room asks

Don't wait to be asked — proactively acknowledge during the presentation that a gender-disaggregated strategy is a next-step design requirement. This shows awareness, preempts the question, and positions you as someone who has thought critically about your own proposal's gaps.

Final Assessment

Where This Proposal Stands

Compared to the original concept, this is a substantially stronger submission. The addition of eight revenue streams, five evidence-backed pilot cities, named academic M&E partners with phase-level KPIs, a three-channel community participation framework, and a principled five-step waste picker integration structure addresses the majority of the original SWOT's weaknesses.

The proposal's remaining vulnerabilities — no rupee figures, no gender strategy, no named team, no legal structure, no technical contingency for digester failure — are real, but they are appropriate gaps for a concept-stage submission. The key is to present them as development priorities, not oversights. That distinction determines whether evaluators see a naive pitch or a rigorous early-stage design.

The proposal's greatest asset going into the presentation is the SWaCH benchmark and the internal logic of the circular loop. If those two ideas land clearly in the opening minutes, the rest of the Q&A will flow from a position of credibility.
